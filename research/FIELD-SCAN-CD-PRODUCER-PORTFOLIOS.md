## 2. Patterns across the field

**What top tier ALWAYS includes:**
- **Client/brand logo strip.** The single most consistent trust signal across CD and editor sites (Mark Baroth, Kele Dobrinski, Matheus, Belén Albiol). Almost nobody skips it once they have 3+ recognizable names. Logos > adjectives — nobody writes "I'm experienced," they show Nike/BMW/Adidas and let the reader infer it.
- **Work as clickable grid → detail page**, not everything embedded on one scroll. Case-study depth (goal, role, outcome) lives one click in, not on the homepage. This is the dominant CD pattern; editors skew more toward reel-first.
- **A single clear positioning line in the hero** — either a title ("Award-winning Marketing") or a one-sentence value prop ("Let's tell your story on social media"), never both a title AND a paragraph bio up top.
- **Contact as a persistent, low-friction CTA** — "Let's talk," a direct email mailto, or a contact-page link in nav. Never a form-only wall.

**Metrics — how the field handles them (or doesn't):**
- Quantified performance metrics (view counts, engagement %, revenue lift) are **rare on portfolio homepages** — findings above show 8 of 11 deep-read sites show *zero* numbers on the page. Metrics live in resumes/case studies, not the visual grid.
- The few that do use numbers (Ayelet Golb's stats block, resume guidance broadly) keep them small and secondary to the reel/case study, framed as context not the headline.
- Where metrics appear at all, they're framed as **outcomes tied to a named campaign** ("25% engagement lift," "12% MoM conversion growth" — per resume-convention research), never as raw platform vanity numbers detached from a client story.

**Case studies with results:** Present on CD-tier sites (Mark Baroth's 7 named case studies), largely absent on freelance editor/social-creator sites, which lean on reel + testimonial instead.

**Press mentions:** Rare in this sample — only appeared as a category label ("Publications" on Davis Hilton), not as embedded press logos or pull-quotes. Not a field norm at this tier (more common one level up, at agency-ECD sites, which this scan didn't target).

**Testimonials:** The default social-proof mechanism for solo/freelance creators without big-name logos yet (Anna Hinkle: 6 testimonials carry the entire site, zero visual case studies). Named, titled clients ("Lauren, Winery Owner") read as more credible than anonymous quotes.

**Resume/CV handling:** Inconsistent — most sites (8/11 read) have **no visible resume link at all**; credibility is carried by the portfolio itself. The few with resumes treat it as a secondary nav item (Monique Bonds), not a hero CTA. None of the sample embedded the resume inline or made it a prominent button — Miles's "About button → resume download" placement is already ahead of the median here.

**Contact/CTA conventions:** "Let's talk" / "Get in touch" / direct mailto is nearly universal. Multiple sites stack 2-3 CTA touchpoints (header button + footer + social icons) rather than relying on one.

**Personal/off-hours identity alongside professional work:** **Not found in this sample at all.** Every site scanned is single-lane — professional persona only, no parallel creative identity (music, art, second craft) shown adjacent to brand work. This is a genuine field gap, not just an oversight on a couple of sites.

**Resume angle (what CD/producer resumes emphasize, mirrored in portfolios):**
- Portfolio link in the resume header is treated as **non-optional** in 2026 guidance — the two documents are expected to work as a pair.
- Metrics phrased as **business outcomes** (budget size owned, campaigns beating target, % lifts), not raw counts — the opposite of platform-native vanity numbers.
- Named brands/agencies carry more resume weight than job titles alone — same logic as the logo-strip pattern in the sites themselves.
- One page is the norm under 10 years' experience; two pages only if every line is job-relevant.
- 2026-specific addition: **AI-tool fluency is now an expected resume line** for CD roles (genAI in production workflows, not just experimentation).

## 3. Gap analysis vs Miles's site

1. **No brand/client logo strip.** This is the single most universal pattern in the field (present on nearly every CD/editor site scanned) and Miles's site has none. His `portfolio` data already encodes the brand handles per playlist (`@adobevideo`, `@adobe`, `@adobecreativecloud`, `@photoshop`, `@upworthy`, etc.) — a logo strip (Adobe, Photoshop, Premiere Pro, Upworthy, Gatorade) could be assembled from data he already has, no new facts needed. **Biggest single gap.**
2. **His per-video play-count density is a genuine differentiator — almost nobody in the field shows this.** 8 of 11 sites read in depth show zero quantified metrics on the visual portfolio itself; the field convention is to keep numbers out of the browsing experience entirely. Miles's site showing exact plays (728.4K, 1.5M, 1.4M) per reel, computed cumulative totals, is *more* data-transparent than the field norm — worth keeping and even leaning into as a stated differentiator, not hiding.
3. **No case-study depth (goal → role → outcome) on any playlist.** The CD tier's dominant pattern is grid-to-detail-page with context; his site currently has role line + brand handles + play count but no "why this existed / what the goal was" framing per playlist. A one-line goal/context string per event (not a rewrite of locked copy — an addition) would close this without touching his hero/nav/CTA.
4. **His jazz "Off the Clock" section is a real point of distinction — the field doesn't do this.** Zero sites in this scan showed a parallel personal creative identity next to brand work. This is worth protecting/amplifying, not just keeping — almost nobody else can do this move credibly (most CDs/producers don't have a second serious craft), and it's already built.
5. **No testimonials.** Given his brand-side, on-camera role, a short quote or two (a producer/creative lead he worked with, or an on-camera "he made this easy" line) would match the field's default trust mechanism for people without a name-brand agency title yet — cheap to add, doesn't touch locked copy.
6. **No explicit case-study "results" framing tied to metrics** (e.g., "co-created X, grew to Y views across the campaign") — the field's metric convention wraps numbers in an outcome narrative; his current per-reel plays are raw and uncontextualized by comparison. Low-risk addition: one framing sentence at the top of the Work section, not per-row.
7. **Resume placement is already ahead of median** — most field sites have no visible resume link at all; his About-button download is a genuine advantage, not a gap. Worth noting as a strength, not fixing.
8. **No press/media mentions section** — minor gap, lowest priority; not a strong field norm at this tier either, so this is optional polish rather than a real hole.

## 4. Resume-mirrors-portfolio angle (for his resume work specifically)

- Field expects the resume to **link straight to the portfolio in the header** — confirm his resume does this prominently (skill: resume-tailoring already builds this).
- Numbers on the resume should be **outcome-framed** ("grew reach across N campaigns to X views," "co-produced N Adobe MAX/NAB/Summit event series") rather than a bare cumulative plays figure — the raw total works beautifully *on the site* (data-density differentiator) but reads better on paper wrapped in a business-outcome sentence.
- Brand names (Adobe, Photoshop, Premiere Pro, Gatorade, Upworthy) should appear as a **named list on the resume**, mirroring the logo-strip gap above — same fix serves both documents.
